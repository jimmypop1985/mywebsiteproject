Describing the content and layout for each page.

In the UK, there is inherent copyright on intellectual property, including websites, meaning that whether or not you put the copyright symbol on a piece of original work, legally it is protected as your own. [12] Copyright protects your work from being copied, rented, distributed, shown, adapted or put on the internet without your permission. In some instances you will use images taken from the internet, for example, from a website, you must get permission or use open source images. You may also need a license to use work that is still free. In short, works on the internet are not in the public domain.

Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them.

“The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect.” -Tim Berners-Lee, W3C Director and inventor of the World Wide Web

Web accessibility means that any sites, tools, or applications that end up on the web need to be designed in a way that caters for people who have disabilities, are older, live in rural areas or developing countries can easily use them. A simple example would be that people who are visually impaired may need larger text or text-to-speech available to them in a site that allows them to use the site properly. Captions or subtitles can help those who are hard of hearing to understand video content. Having a clear contrast between foregrounds and backgrounds will assist those with poor contrast sensitivity, a common issue for people who are older. People with physical disabilities, for example, might need to use voice recognition software to interact with a computer. Accessibility issues must be considered during any development process as it is an essential aspect of the world wide web. [8]

Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites

“The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect.” -Tim Berners-Lee, W3C Director and inventor of the World Wide Web

There are lots of different types of files out there, often multiple types for the same thing, for example an image could be a jpeg or a png. This must be considered when developing a site as some filetypes might not be appropriate for what they are used for, for example, a .wav filetype is typically an uncompressed sound filetype and might take up a lot of space when downloaded whereas and mp3 file format is compressed and takes up less space. Some filetypes may need extra software to be downloaded to work, again this must be considered and perhaps an alternative filetype chosen. See some examples of different types of files below.[9]
